Basotho attire is outlined by the colorful, patterned Basotho blankets known as Seana Marena, the traditional dresses known as Seshoeshoe for woman and the recognizable canibal straw hat known as Mokorotlo etc. These clothes are rich in cultural symbolism, showcasing identity, heritage and social status and are worn traditionally in the time of ceremonies, festivals and daily activities, especially in the cold Lesotho climate.

Basotho hats, also known as MOKOROTLO are iconic symbol of Basotho culture and heritage in Lesotho. These recognizable hats have a rich history and are deeply woven into the identity of Basotho people.

Historical Context The origins of the Mokorotlo are somewhat unclear, but it is believed that the Sotho people may have adopted the hat from the Cape Malays, who wore similar styles. The hat gained prominence in the early 20th century when chiefs began to wear it as a symbol of leadership and authority. Over time, new designs emerged to cater to women, expanding its use beyond traditional male gatherings

Seshoeshoe, or shweshwe, is a printed cotton fabric that forms the basis of traditional attire for Basotho women. The fabric comes in vibrant colors and intricate designs, and it is used to make dresses, skirts, and blouses.

Seshoeshoe is mostly worn by Basotho woman during ceremonies, festivals and in their everyday life.Shweshwe is produced where cotton fabric is fed through copper rollers which have patterns etched into them. A weak acid solution is fed onto the fabric, bleaching out the distinctive intricate white designs

Beadwork is the art or craft of attaching beads to one another, typically by stringing them onto a thread or thin wire, or sewing them onto fabric. Beads can be made from various materials and come in different shapes and sizes, allowing for a wide range of artistic expression.

. Common forms of beadwork include bead stringing, bead weaving, and bead embroidery, often used in fabric decoration and cultural storytelling. This craft has historical significance, with glass beads used decoratively in ancient civilization.

Moqhaka is a traditional Basotho headgear, it is usually made from animal skin and decorated with feathers or other symbolic elements. It is worn by Basotho Chiefs or leaders, men participating in cultural or initiation ceremonies. It identifies symbol of status, power and identity.

A thethana consists of a waistband upon which individually braided strands of fiber are attached. Historically, as a South Sotho girl reached the age of four or five she was typically dressed in a thethana, as it was often the only garment worn by young girls throughout childhood until the onset of puberty up until the early 20th century.

As the girl advanced in age and social development, she would be given new lithethana (plural of thethana), the length of which increased with the corresponding status of the wearer.
